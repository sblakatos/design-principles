What is Closure?

Textbook Definition

"We mentally fill in the gaps in order to complete a perceived shape...The idea of designing with a only a part but having your viewer perceive the whole opens up interesting compositional opportunities, including the interplay of positive and negative space"(pg 62).

White Space is Not Your Enemy by Rebecca Hagen and Kim Golombisky. Published in 2017 by Taylor and Francis Group, LLC.

Breaking It Down

Integrating an incomplete shape within your layout can add an element of excitement for the reader. Our brains allow us to make out shapes even if they are incomplete. It would also be a unique way to encompass an element of contrast within your layout because of the negative and positive space being used.

Real World Example

This artwork is a nice example of closure. It has a nice balance of positive and negative space. The negative space can be filled in the person looking at it.

If the artwork was only positive space, it would lose its impact, leaving nothing up for interpretation. Allowing the person to fill in the blanks on their own, adds a level of interest to the painting.
